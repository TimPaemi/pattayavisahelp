Non-Immigrant M (Media) Visa — for foreign correspondents and film crew

For accredited foreign journalists, correspondents, and film production crews. Strict requirements: registered media entity (6+ months), 12 pieces of content/year, no commercial side income.

Are you a YouTuber or content creator? Almost certainly the wrong visa. Non-M requires formal affiliation with a registered media entity. Independent YouTubers don't qualify. The DTV is the right visa for content creators earning foreign income.

Eligibility

  • Affiliated with credible media entity registered for 6+ months (newspaper, broadcaster, online news platform)
  • Employed solely as media professional — no other commercial activity
  • Discharge media duties 90+ days/year, produce 12+ pieces of content annually
  • Clean record (no threats to public order)
  • Demonstrable media role: journalist, correspondent, documentary director, film crew

Application via MMOS portal

The MFA Media Online Service (mmos.mfa.go.th) is the mandatory portal for stays of 90+ days:

  1. Register on MMOS
  2. Media entity HQ submits letter to Director-General, Department of Information, MFA
  3. Upload passport, CV, company profile, registration proof, portfolio (10–12 pieces), criminal clearance
  4. MFA reviews (15–30 working days) → issues Letter of Authorisation
  5. Apply for Non-M visa at Royal Thai Embassy abroad or e-Visa with auth number
  6. Receive 90-day visa; on arrival in Thailand, apply for 1-year extension + press card / work permit

Pattaya angle

Pattaya has a sizeable foreign content creator community — but most are not on Non-M visas. Genuine accredited foreign press (BBC stringers, freelance documentary makers, registered news outlet correspondents) qualify. Lifestyle vloggers, travel YouTubers, and influencers should use DTV.

Frequently asked questions

Can I do sponsored brand partnerships on Non-M?Legally risky. Non-M accredits you for journalism, not commercial advertising. Brand collaborations may require separate work permit.
What's the 12 pieces of content rule?For renewal: 12 pieces of published content per year about Thailand or the region. Falling short = renewal refusal.

Deep dive

Non-M Media visa: who actually qualifies and how to apply

The Non-Immigrant M (Media) visa is for accredited journalists, news photographers, documentary filmmakers, foreign correspondents, and content creators producing material about Thailand for international audiences. It's one of the rarer Non-Immigrant categories and the application is genuinely scrutinized.

Who qualifies

  • Journalists employed by foreign news organisations on assignment in Thailand
  • Foreign correspondents based in Thailand for ongoing coverage
  • Photojournalists with verified outlet affiliation
  • Documentary filmmakers with PR Department approval for the production
  • Content creators with substantial reach producing material specifically about Thailand

Who doesn't qualify (despite trying)

  • YouTubers without proven audience size or business structure
  • Bloggers with personal websites not affiliated with media organizations
  • Travel vloggers using Thailand as just one of many destinations
  • Freelance writers without a verifiable assignment letter
  • Anyone whose "media work" produces no published output

Most independent content creators are better served by the DTV under the soft-power or remote-work categories, which has dramatically lower documentation barriers.

The PR Department gateway

The Government Public Relations Department (PR Department) is the agency that vets media activities in Thailand. For substantial media work — documentary filming, foreign news bureau setup, ongoing coverage — you need PR Department accreditation BEFORE applying for the visa. The accreditation involves submitting your project, credentials, and intended output for review.

Application path

  1. Get PR Department accreditation letter (4–8 weeks for documentary projects)
  2. Apply at Thai embassy abroad with: PR letter, employer assignment letter, press credentials, passport, financial proof
  3. Single-entry Non-M is typically valid 90 days; multiple-entry up to 1 year
  4. Annual extension at Immigration in Thailand requires fresh PR Department endorsement

What changed in 2024-2026

Thailand has tightened press accreditation in recent years, partly in response to coverage perceived as critical. Foreign correspondents covering politically sensitive topics report longer review times for PR Department accreditation. Documentary filmmakers covering tourism, business, or cultural topics generally get faster turnarounds.

Pattaya media landscape

Pattaya hosts a small foreign media community — primarily Thai-based tourism journalists and a handful of independent documentary filmmakers covering crime, nightlife, or expat community stories. Bangkok hosts most accredited foreign correspondents and the Foreign Correspondents Club of Thailand (FCCT).

Financial requirements

No standard income requirement, but expect to show enough financial standing to support yourself during the visa period. Most applicants show employer income, savings, or both. ฿500,000 in liquid assets is a reasonable benchmark.

Working scope

The Non-M permits journalism and media production for the specific outlet you're accredited with. Side gigs (commercial photography, freelance for other outlets, paid sponsored content) require separate work permission or are not permitted under Non-M.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Can a YouTuber or social media creator get a Non-M visa? In theory yes, in practice it's very difficult. You need verifiable proof you produce ongoing media content about Thailand for substantial audiences, AND a Thai company or sponsoring outlet, AND PR Department accreditation. Most creators with under 100k subscribers are better off with the DTV soft-power category.
How long does PR Department accreditation take? 4–8 weeks for documentary projects, longer for politically sensitive topics. Some news bureau setups take 3+ months. Time it carefully if your project has deadlines.
Can I work on a Non-M for a Thai news outlet? Yes, but you also need a work permit. The Non-M makes you eligible; the work permit (sponsored by the Thai outlet) makes your work legal. Many Thai-based foreign journalists have both a Non-M visa and a Thai-employer-sponsored work permit.
Is the Non-M renewable indefinitely? Yes, with annual extensions and continuing PR Department endorsement. Some long-term foreign correspondents have been on Non-M for 10+ years. The renewal hinges on your continuing media affiliation.
What about freelance documentary filmmakers? Possible but harder. You need a Thai production company or international production company sponsoring you, plus PR Department approval for the specific film. Solo independent filmmakers without affiliation rarely qualify.
How does Non-M compare to DTV for content creators? DTV is faster, cheaper, less scrutinized — designed for remote workers and soft-power participants. Non-M has more access (press conferences, official events) but heavier paperwork. Most independent creators choose DTV; only those genuinely embedded in the press ecosystem need Non-M.
Can I apply for Non-M from inside Thailand? No. Like most Non-Immigrant categories, you must apply from a Thai embassy outside Thailand. Common application points for media: Vientiane, Phnom Penh, Singapore, Hong Kong.
What's the visa fee? Standard Non-Immigrant rates: ฿2,000 single-entry, ฿5,000 multiple-entry. PR Department accreditation processing is free. Practical costs (translations, courier, travel) typically ฿8,000–฿15,000 on top.
Can my spouse and kids come on dependent visas? Yes — Non-O dependent visas for spouse and children under 20 are available, similar to other Non-Immigrant categories. Dependents cannot work on the dependent visa.
Where do I find PR Department contacts? Government Public Relations Department, headquartered in Bangkok. Foreign correspondent assistance is handled by the Foreign Correspondents Liaison Section. The Foreign Correspondents Club of Thailand (FCCT) in Bangkok is also a useful gateway for new foreign correspondents.
