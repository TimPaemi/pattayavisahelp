Non-O-A vs Non-O — what's the difference?

Non-O-A: Applied at Thai Embassy in your home country. Get 1-year visa upfront. Health insurance mandatory (40k THB OPD / 400k THB IPD minimum). Police clearance + medical certificate required.

Non-O (in-country): Applied at Jomtien Immigration after arrival. No mandatory insurance. No police clearance. No medical cert. Same financial requirements (800k THB).

Most Pattaya retirees, especially over 65, choose Non-O in-country due to insurance economics. See Non-O page · See full comparison.

Eligibility

  • Aged 50+ on day of application
  • Passport with 18+ months validity
  • Police clearance from home country (FBI / ACPO / ACIC)
  • Medical certificate confirming no prohibited diseases
  • Health insurance: 40,000 THB OPD + 400,000 THB IPD minimum (some embassies enforce higher)
  • 800,000 THB Thai bank deposit OR 65,000 THB/month income OR combination
  • Employment of any kind strictly prohibited

Application process

  1. Open Thai bank account, deposit 800k (allow 2 months seasoning)
  2. Obtain police clearance from home country (4–8 weeks)
  3. Obtain medical certificate from licensed doctor
  4. Purchase qualifying health insurance policy
  5. Submit application at Royal Thai Embassy in your country
  6. Receive 1-year multiple-entry Non-O-A visa
  7. Enter Thailand; receive 1-year permission-to-stay stamp
  8. Annual extensions at Jomtien thereafter (1,900 THB)

Total lead time first year: 6–10 weeks (police clearance is usually the bottleneck).

Deep dive

O-A vs Non-O retirement: which one fits your situation

The Non-Immigrant O-A visa is one of two retirement-visa pathways for foreigners 50+. It differs from the more common Non-O Retirement on three big points: where you apply, mandatory health insurance, and the multiple-entry structure.

The three differences that matter

  • O-A is applied for in your home country at a Thai embassy — not in Thailand. Non-O can be applied for inside Thailand if you're already there on visa-exempt or another visa.
  • Health insurance is mandatory for O-A: minimum ฿40,000 outpatient + ฿400,000 inpatient annual coverage with a Thai-approved insurer. Non-O has no insurance requirement.
  • O-A is multiple-entry, valid 1 year from issue, with each entry granting 1 year of stay. This means you can effectively get 2 years from one O-A visa by entering on the last day of validity. Non-O extensions are 1 year each.

Financial requirement

Same as Non-O Retirement: ฿800,000 in a Thai bank account, OR ฿65,000/month verified income, OR a combination totalling ฿800k annually. The bank account must be in YOUR name (not joint with a Thai spouse) and seasoned 2 months for the first application, 3 months for subsequent extensions.

Health insurance specifics (the catch)

The mandatory health insurance for O-A is one of the friction points that makes some retirees prefer Non-O instead. Requirements:

  • Minimum ฿40,000 outpatient + ฿400,000 inpatient annual coverage
  • Insurer must be on the approved list (Thai general insurers + select international ones)
  • Pre-existing conditions are typically excluded for the first year
  • Annual premiums for healthy 60-year-old: ฿20,000–฿80,000/year depending on coverage level
  • Annual premiums for 70+ retirees: ฿80,000–฿200,000/year, sometimes more

O-A application path

  1. Get health insurance certificate from approved insurer (3 months valid)
  2. Get police clearance certificate from your home country (FBI for US, ACRO for UK, Bundeszentralregister for Germany, etc.)
  3. Get medical certificate stating you don't have prohibited diseases
  4. Show financial proof: ฿800k in home-country or Thai bank, or ฿65k/month income
  5. Apply at Thai embassy in your home country (NOT a third country)
  6. Receive multiple-entry O-A valid 1 year
  7. Enter Thailand within validity — each entry gives 1 year of stay

Pattaya O-A holders: what works

O-A holders in Pattaya often use the same insurance for years (typically through Pacific Cross, Aetna Thailand, or a similar approved insurer). Renewal year 2 onward is at Thai immigration as a Non-O extension — once you're in the country, the rules effectively merge with Non-O. You don't keep applying for O-A; you transition to annual Non-O extensions.

Why some 50+ retirees still choose O-A

  • You're applying from your home country and want to arrive with a clean 2-year buffer
  • You want the mandatory insurance forced — keeps you covered automatically
  • Your insurance situation already meets the requirement, so it's "free"
  • You want to enter and exit Thailand multiple times in year 1 without re-entry permits (multi-entry visa allows this)

Why others avoid O-A and go for Non-O

  • Insurance requirement is genuinely expensive at 65+
  • Pre-existing conditions excluded from approved insurers' policies
  • Already in Thailand and don't want to leave for embassy application
  • Police clearance from home country is paperwork they don't want

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between O-A and O-X? O-A is 1 year multiple-entry. O-X is 10 years total (two 5-year periods). O-X requires ฿3,000,000 in Thai bank (vs ฿800k for O-A). O-A insurance is annual; O-X requires the insurance for the full 10 years upfront-able commitment.
Can I apply for O-A from a third country? No. O-A must be applied for at a Thai embassy in your country of nationality or country of legal residence. You cannot fly to Vientiane and get an O-A — that's a Non-O path. You can fly to your home country, apply, then return.
What insurance providers count for the O-A requirement? Thai general insurers like Pacific Cross, Aetna, AXA Thailand, Bupa Thailand are all approved. Some international insurers like Cigna Global, William Russell, and IMG provide certificates accepted by Thai embassies. Check the official Thai General Insurance Association list before you commit.
Are pre-existing conditions covered? Generally no. Approved O-A insurers exclude pre-existing conditions for the first 12 months. Some have permanent exclusions. If you have significant chronic conditions, you may not be able to get the required coverage at any price — at which point Non-O Retirement (no insurance requirement) becomes the alternative.
Does the O-A allow me to work? No. Like all retirement visas, O-A explicitly prohibits work in Thailand. If you want to work, switch to Non-B or LTR (Wealthy Pensioner with Work Permit eligibility).
Can my non-Thai spouse come on a dependent visa? Yes — your spouse can apply for a Non-O dependent visa. They don't need to be 50+. Children under 20 can also apply for Non-O dependents. Dependent visas don't require the financial requirement separately.
How does the 90-day reporting work for O-A? Same as any other long-stay visa. Every 90 days you're in Thailand you must report your address. Online, by mail, in person at Jomtien — your choice. Read our 90-day reporting guide for methods.
Can I switch from O-A to Privilege Visa? Yes. Many retirees move from O-A to Privilege after 1–2 years to escape the annual insurance and financial proof routine. Privilege Visa is one-time payment (฿650k–฿2.5M) for 5–20 years stay, no annual financial proof, no insurance requirement, fast-track airport service.
What's the realistic total cost in year 1? Visa fee at embassy: ฿5,000. Police clearance from home country: $50–$150. Medical certificate: $50–$200. Approved insurance: ฿20k–฿200k depending on age. Translations and courier: $100–$300. Total realistic: ฿40,000–฿250,000 first year, then ฿20k–฿200k annually for insurance + ฿1,900 extension.
Is the O-A path more secure than Non-O? Marginally. O-A has insurance built in, which protects you if hospitalized. The visa itself isn't more 'secure' — both are subject to the same Thai immigration rules. Real security comes from doing the paperwork correctly and not overstaying.
