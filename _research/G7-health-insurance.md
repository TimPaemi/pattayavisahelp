## When health insurance is mandatory vs optional

In 2026, Thailand requires health insurance for some long-stay visas, while for others it is technically optional but strongly recommended.

### Mandatory (visa won't be approved without qualifying insurance)

- **Non-Immigrant O-A (Retirement, applied at embassy):**
  - **40,000 THB outpatient (OPD) + 400,000 THB inpatient (IPD)** — unchanged minimums (thaiembassy+2)
  - Many insurers now offer O-A–compliant policies up to 3,000,000–4,000,000 THB coverage (axa.co)
- **Non-Immigrant O-X (10-year Retirement):**
  - At least **3,000,000 THB inpatient coverage** is generally applied as the practical minimum; some guidelines still reference 400,000/40,000 but practice has shifted to higher limits for 10-year stays (issacompass+1)
  - Policies must cover the entire 5-year period or be renewable annually to cover it (pacificprime+1)
- **LTR Visa (all categories):**
  - **Minimum USD 50,000 medical coverage per year**, OR Thai social security, OR a deposit of USD 100,000 (hlbthai+2)
- **Thailand Privilege (Elite) packages (most tiers):**
  - **Minimum USD 10,000 medical coverage** for treatment in Thailand, including COVID where still stipulated (pacificprime)
- **Some Non-B (employment) cases:**
  - Insurance may be employer-mandated (company group plan) but is not a blanket Immigration rule; BOI/LTR/SMART structures can impose their own requirements (issacompass+1)
- **Some ED/University students:**
  - University-provided coverage is often accepted; private language students may be asked to show personal coverage depending on school policy (thailandexpatservices)

### Not legally mandatory (as of April 2026), but strongly recommended

- **Non-Immigrant O (in-country retirement extension):** No official health insurance requirement; many retirees still choose private cover due to rising healthcare costs (thaiembassy+1)
- **Non-O Marriage** (Thai spouse)
- **Standard Non-B employment visas** (non-BOI, non-LTR) — though employers often provide social security/public healthcare; private top-ups recommended (expatica)
- **DTV (Destination Thailand Visa)** — no mandatory insurance requirement in the published framework; private cover strongly advised due to long stays and remote-worker profiles (smartlifethailand)
- **SMART Visa** — no explicit health-insurance minimum in public BOI criteria; many employers and BOI partners strongly encourage private cover (thailand.acclime+1)

## Category-by-category minimums (2026)

### 1. Non-Immigrant O-A (Retirement, 1-year, applied abroad)

- **Minimum coverage** (official guideline, still cited and enforced):
  - OPD: **40,000 THB**
  - IPD: **400,000 THB** (longstay.tgia+3)
- Policy must cover the entire visa validity period (1 year) (longstay.tgia+2)
- **COVID-19 coverage:** the 2020–2022 COVID-specific requirement has been dropped; policies no longer need specific COVID wording, but must cover general inpatient treatment (thailandexpatservices+1)
- **Accepted providers:** OIC-approved Thai insurers and foreign insurers whose policies meet the THB thresholds and provide a Foreign Insurance Certificate in the MFA format (longstay.tgia+1)

### 2. Non-Immigrant O-X (10-year Retirement)

- **Practice has evolved:**
  - Many embassies and insurers now apply **3,000,000 THB inpatient coverage** as the practical minimum for O-X, reflecting the longer 5+5-year horizon (issacompass+1)
  - Some embassy pages still reference the earlier 400,000/40,000 THB thresholds, but updated advisory sources stress higher coverage for O-X (pacificprime+1)
- Policy must cover medical expenses in Thailand for **at least 5 years** (or be renewable to cover that entire period) (consular.mfa+1)
- Health insurance from foreign companies may be accepted if it meets Thai standards and is supported by the Foreign Insurance Certificate (pacificprime)

### 3. LTR (Long-Term Resident) Visa

- All four LTR categories (W, P, T, H) require:
  - Health insurance covering **at least USD 50,000 medical expenses per year**; OR
  - Thai social security rights that ensure coverage in Thailand; OR
  - A Thai or foreign bank deposit of **USD 100,000** in lieu of an insurance policy (hlbthai+1)
- Policy must be valid for at least **10 months** at the time of application (axa.co+1)
- Many LTR-specific plans from Thai insurers (e.g., AXA EasyCare Visa) explicitly reference LTR compliance in their marketing material (insurance-thailand+1)

### 4. Thailand Privilege (Elite)

- Health insurance is **mandatory for new Thailand Privilege (Elite) members** in most tiers:
  - **Minimum coverage: USD 10,000 medical costs per policy year**, including hospitalization (pacificprime)
  - COVID coverage may still be asked for some packages but is no longer a universal requirement
- Members can use Thai or international policies as long as they meet the minimums and can provide an English or Thai insurance certificate (pacificprime)

### 5. DTV (Destination Thailand Visa)

- **No official, globally-applied medical insurance minimum** is published as a condition for DTV approval as of April 2026; instead:
  - Health insurance is **strongly recommended** in official and advisory materials, especially for remote workers and nomads expecting to rely on private hospitals (smartlifethailand+1)
  - Some consular posts may ask for evidence of ability to cover medical expenses (bank statements, insurance) as part of their risk assessment
- Many brokers sell DTV-specific insurance bundles (typically 1–5 years, 1–3 million THB coverage) (insurance-thailand)

### 6. Non-B (Business / Work)

- Immigration doesn't impose a blanket private-insurance requirement, but:
  - Employees contributing to the Thai social security system gain access to public healthcare in designated hospitals (expatica)
  - Many companies, especially BOI-promoted or white-collar employers, provide private group health insurance as a benefit; coverage varies widely (thailandexpatservices+1)
- For Non-B dependents (Non-O based on spouse/child) there is often no mandatory insurance, though private cover is strongly advised (issacompass)

### 7. Non-O (in-country retirement) and Non-O Marriage

- **As of April 2026, no official health insurance requirement applies** to in-country Non-O retirement extensions or Non-O based on marriage (thaiembassy+1)
- ThaiEmbassy.com and other legal sources confirm: **the 2019–2021 retirement insurance rules apply specifically to O-A visas, not to Non-O in-country extensions** (thaiembassy)
- Some immigration offices occasionally encourage or informally nudge retirees to show insurance at the counter, but it is not a legal requirement for Non-O extensions in 2026 (thailandexpatservices+1)

## Thai vs international insurance: pros and cons

### Thai insurers

**Pros:**

- Plans explicitly designed to match Thai visa requirements (O-A, O-X, LTR, Privilege) (axa.co+1)
- Pre-approved by Immigration and embassies; standardized certificate formats via TGIA (longstay.tgia)
- Direct billing relationships with major Thai hospitals (Bangkok Hospital, Bumrungrad, Bangkok Pattaya, etc.) (expatica+1)
- Premiums often more affordable for mid-range coverage than international plans at ages 50–70 (wise)

**Cons:**

- Coverage is often Thailand-only or region-limited
- Strict age caps for new applicants (often 70–75) (expatica+1)
- Narrower coverage for pre-existing conditions and more frequent sub-limits (e.g. caps per condition, per year) (thailandexpatservices+1)
- Documentation mostly in Thai; English certificates provided but policy wordings can be complex

### International insurers

**Pros:**

- Worldwide coverage (including home country or entire Asia region) (wise+1)
- Higher annual limits (USD 1–5 million or more) and comprehensive benefits (expatica)
- Flexible deductibles and co-pay structures
- Some accept new applicants at older ages than Thai insurers (expatica)

**Cons:**

- Significantly higher premiums for older age bands
- Must ensure the policy meets specific Thai thresholds and get a Foreign Insurance Certificate; otherwise embassies may reject it (issacompass+1)
- Some Thai hospitals may require you to pay and claim back rather than direct bill

## Common gaps and traps

1. **Pre-existing condition exclusions:** Many visa-compliant policies exclude or heavily limit coverage for pre-existing conditions (cardiac issues, diabetes, cancer history). Retirees often assume old conditions will be covered and only discover limitations at claim time. **Always ask explicitly how your known conditions are treated.** (smartlifethailand+1)
2. **Age caps for new applicants:** Many Thai plans won't accept new clients over 70 or 75, even though they will renew existing policyholders. **Older newcomers must either accept higher-priced international plans or pivot to the Non-O in-country route that doesn't require insurance.** (smartlifethailand+1)
3. **Inpatient sub-limits:** Some cheaper plans technically meet the minimum IPD threshold but include sub-limits like:
   - 50,000 THB per surgery
   - 20,000 THB per day room limit
   These can result in large out-of-pocket bills at top-tier hospitals (smartlifethailand+1)
4. **Annual deductible trap:** Lower premiums often come with high deductibles (e.g. 100,000–300,000 THB per year). These can wipe out any savings for retirees who actually use the system and expect to have claims (axa.co+1)
5. **Duration mismatch:** Policies must cover the entire visa period (1 year for O-A, 5 years initial for O-X/LTR, etc.). Some applicants buy a 6-month policy to cut cost and are rejected because coverage duration is too short (issacompass+1)
6. **COVID coverage misunderstanding:** COVID-specific coverage requirements have largely been removed for most visa categories, but some advisory sites still mention them. As of 2025–2026, O-A/O-X/LTR generally require standard inpatient coverage; COVID is treated like any other illness as long as IPD coverage is adequate (thailandexpatservices+1)

## 12 FAQ questions

**1. Do I need health insurance for a Non-O in-country retirement extension?**
**No.** As of 2026, health insurance is not legally required for Non-O in-country retirement extensions; the mandatory insurance applies to O-A visas. However, private insurance is strongly recommended, especially for 60+ retirees. (thaiembassy+2)

**2. What are the exact insurance requirements for a Non-O-A visa?**
You must have at least **40,000 THB outpatient and 400,000 THB inpatient** coverage, for the entire visa period. The policy can be from a Thai or foreign insurer, but foreign policies must prove coverage via a specific certificate. (thaiembassy+2)

**3. What are the insurance requirements for the O-X 10-year visa?**
You need long-term health insurance covering medical treatment in Thailand, **commonly 3,000,000 THB IPD in current practice**, for at least 5 years or renewable to cover that period. Some guidance still references 400,000/40,000, but embassies and brokers increasingly treat O-X as requiring higher cover. (consular.mfa+2)

**4. What insurance does the LTR visa require?**
LTR applicants must show health insurance covering **at least USD 50,000 per year**, or Thai social security covering treatment in Thailand, or a USD 100,000 bank deposit. The policy must be valid for at least 10 months at application. (hlbthai+2)

**5. Is health insurance mandatory for Thailand Privilege (Elite)?**
Yes, new Privilege applicants must show coverage of **at least USD 10,000 per year for medical care in Thailand**, including hospitalisation. Specific package details vary, but this is the common baseline. (pacificprime)

**6. I'm over 70 and can't find an affordable Thai plan — what are my options?**
Options include: international plans with higher premiums; **Non-O in-country retirement path (no insurance requirement)**; or LTR-P if you meet income thresholds and can secure acceptable international cover. Work with a broker familiar with over-70 underwriting. (issacompass+2)

**7. Does my policy need to cover COVID-19 specifically?**
Generally no, as of 2025–2026 the explicit COVID coverage requirement has been dropped. Policies just need to meet the standard IPD/OPD or USD 50k thresholds. Some embassies may still ask for confirmation that pandemic illnesses are not excluded. (thailandexpatservices+2)

**8. Can I use travel insurance for my O-A or LTR visa?**
**Short-term travel insurance is rarely accepted.** Visa categories requiring insurance expect long-term health insurance (annual or multi-year policies) with clear inpatient and outpatient coverage, not 30–90-day travel policies. (axa.co+1)

**9. Do I need insurance for a tourist visa or visa-exempt entry?**
No mandatory requirement, but high-quality private insurance is strongly recommended, especially for longer tourist stays or if you plan to use private hospitals. (smartlifethailand+1)

**10. Will Thai hospitals treat me if I have no insurance?**
Yes, but you'll be billed in full and may have to pay upfront for non-emergency care. Without insurance, major procedures (cardiac, cancer, ICU) can cost hundreds of thousands or millions of THB. (expatica)

**11. How do I prove my insurance to Immigration or an embassy?**
Typically:
- Copy of your policy (English or Thai)
- Insurer-issued certificate of coverage summarising limits and validity
- For foreign insurers, a **Foreign Insurance Certificate in the standard Thai format** is often required for O-A/O-X (issacompass+2)

**12. Can I switch insurers mid-visa period?**
Yes, but you must ensure there is no gap in coverage and that your new policy meets the visa's requirements. At renewal, Immigration or the embassy will check that your coverage is continuous and compliant. (thailandexpatservices+1)

## 2025–2026 enforcement changes

- **O-A coverage level harmonised at 400k/40k for medical, 3M COVID requirement removed:** Updated guidance confirms that the COVID-specific requirement is no longer part of the O-A baseline; standard 400k/40k remains (pacificprime+1)
- **O-X and LTR emphasise higher coverage:** LTR's USD 50k requirement and O-X's practical shift towards 3M IPD have pushed the market toward higher-coverage plans for long-stay retirees (issacompass+1)
- **International insurers more widely accepted:** Provided they issue the Foreign Insurance Certificate and meet thresholds, more embassies now accept non-Thai policies, widening choice for high-net-worth expats (issacompass+1)
- **Stronger focus on pre-existing conditions:** Advisory content increasingly stresses documentation of pre-existing conditions and how they're treated; some offices may ask for statements showing coverage, especially for over-70 renewals (thailandexpatservices+1)
- **Insurance as a de facto requirement for many expats:** Even where not mandated (Non-O in-country, DTV, SMART), 2026 guidance from brokers and hospitals treats private health insurance as effectively essential for any long-stay foreigner in Thailand due to rising medical costs (wise+2)
