## Critical correction: Thailand is NOT a Hague Apostille signatory

**This single fact causes more confusion than any other in the foreign marriage process.**

Thailand has **not signed the Hague Apostille Convention**. This means:

- Apostille from your home country is **not enough on its own** to make your marriage certificate valid in Thailand
- You **must** also have the document certified by either:
  - The Royal Thai Embassy/Consulate in your home country (before bringing it to Thailand), OR
  - Your home country's embassy in Bangkok (after arriving in Thailand)
- After that, the document must be:
  - Translated into Thai by a certified translator
  - Legalised by Thailand's Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA)
  - Registered at a Thai Amphoe (district office) → Kor Ror 22 form issued

**This 4–6 step process applies to all foreign marriage certificates** regardless of whether your home country issues apostilles or uses other authentication methods.

## Why this matters for Thai visa applications

The **Non-O Marriage visa** requires Thai recognition of your marriage. Without Amphoe registration (Kor Ror 22), Thai Immigration does not consider you legally married for visa purposes — regardless of how legitimate your home-country marriage is.

**The required end-state:** Kor Ror 22 form issued by a Thai Amphoe, confirming your marriage is registered in Thailand's civil registration system.

**The path to get there:** authentication → certification → translation → MFA legalisation → Amphoe registration.

## The standard chain (universal across countries)

Five steps, in order. Skipping any step breaks the chain.

### Step 1 — Home-country authentication

Get your foreign marriage certificate authenticated by the relevant authority in your home country. The exact authority depends on your country (see country-specific sections below).

- **Hague Convention countries** (UK, US, EU, Australia, Canada, etc.): Apostille from your country's designated authority
- **Non-Hague countries** (India, China, etc.): Direct consular legalisation through your country's Ministry of Foreign Affairs

### Step 2 — Royal Thai Embassy certification

After home-country authentication, the document must be certified by either:

- **Royal Thai Embassy or Consulate in your home country** (recommended — easier to do before travelling), OR
- Your country's embassy in Bangkok (alternative if you're already in Thailand)

This step adds a Thai government stamp confirming your foreign authentication is valid.

### Step 3 — Translation to Thai

The certified document must be translated into Thai by a **certified translator** (typically MFA-registered). Translations from non-certified sources may be rejected.

- **Cost in Pattaya:** typically 500–2,000 THB per document
- **Time:** 2–7 working days

### Step 4 — Thai MFA legalisation

The translated document must be legalised by Thailand's **Department of Consular Affairs at the Ministry of Foreign Affairs** in Bangkok.

- **Address:** Department of Consular Affairs, MFA Building, Chaeng Watthana Road, Bangkok
- **Appointment required since 2025:** wait 2–4 weeks
- **Same-day service:** Available with extra fee
- **Standard fee:** 200 THB per document (verify current fee post-April-2026)
- **Walk-in not accepted** — must book appointment

### Step 5 — Amphoe registration → Kor Ror 22

Take the fully legalised document to a Thai Amphoe (district office) to register the foreign marriage in Thailand's civil system.

- **Which Amphoe:** Your Thai spouse's hometown Amphoe (where their Tabien Baan/house registration is filed)
- **Documents needed:** Legalised marriage certificate + Thai translation, both spouses' passports/ID, Thai spouse's Tabien Baan, Thai spouse's ID card
- **Result:** **Kor Ror 22 form** issued — proof of foreign marriage registration in Thailand
- **Cost:** Minimal (administrative fee, typically <100 THB)
- **Time:** Same day or 1–2 working days

## Common pitfalls

1. **Assuming apostille alone is sufficient.** Thailand isn't a Hague signatory. After your home-country apostille, you still need Royal Thai Embassy certification.
2. **Using a non-certified translator.** Translations from random translators or AI tools are rejected. Use MFA-certified translators only.
3. **Skipping the Amphoe registration.** Many couples complete MFA legalisation and stop there — but Thai Immigration requires the **Kor Ror 22** from the Amphoe, not just the MFA-legalised certificate.
4. **Using the wrong Amphoe.** Registration must be at your Thai spouse's hometown Amphoe (where their Tabien Baan is filed), not at any random Amphoe.
5. **Documents older than 1 year.** Some Amphoes prefer recent legalisations. If your home-country authentication is years old, consider re-authenticating.
6. **Not having Thai translations of supporting documents.** Some Amphoes require translations of birth certificates, divorce decrees (if previously married), etc. — beyond just the marriage certificate.
7. **MFA appointment booking lead time.** Since 2025, MFA legalisation requires an appointment. Wait can be 2–4 weeks — book early.
8. **Forgetting the original.** Certified copies aren't enough at some stages. Carry originals and certified copies.

## 12 FAQ questions

**1. Do I really need to register my foreign marriage in Thailand?**
For Non-O Marriage visa purposes — yes, absolutely. Without a Kor Ror 22 from a Thai Amphoe, Thai Immigration doesn't recognise your foreign marriage. For other purposes (joint bank accounts, property co-ownership, etc.), Thai recognition is also typically required.

**2. Can I do the legalisation entirely from Thailand?**
Partially — Steps 3–5 (translation, MFA, Amphoe) are done in Thailand. But Steps 1–2 (home-country authentication + Royal Thai Embassy certification) typically need to be initiated either in your home country or via your country's embassy in Bangkok.

**3. How long does the whole process take?**
**4–8 weeks total** for most countries. UK and Australia are at the faster end (5–8 weeks); US is slower due to state-level apostille variability (6–10 weeks); India and China are slowest due to multi-step home-country authentication (6–10 weeks).

**4. How much does it cost?**
Total cost typically **$50–$200 USD equivalent** plus travel/courier fees. Per-step breakdown: home-country authentication ($5–$50), Royal Thai Embassy ($25–$50), Thai translation ($15–$60), Thai MFA ($5–$10), Amphoe (<$5).

**5. What's the difference between apostille and consular legalisation?**
**Apostille:** A standardised certification under the Hague Convention, accepted by all member countries. **Consular legalisation:** A direct authentication chain through specific embassies, used for non-Hague countries. **Thailand isn't in the Hague Convention** — so even apostille-issuing countries' documents need additional Royal Thai Embassy certification.

**6. Can a Pattaya agency handle the whole process for me?**
Some Pattaya legal/translation agencies offer end-to-end service (5,000–15,000 THB inclusive) covering Steps 3–5 in Thailand. **Steps 1–2 still need to start in your home country** unless you use your country's embassy in Bangkok.

**7. We were married in a small town with non-standard documentation — is that a problem?**
The marriage certificate must be the official legal document recognised by your home government. Religious-only ceremonies, common-law arrangements, or unregistered marriages typically can't be legalised. Confirm your home-country marriage is officially registered before starting.

**8. We're a same-sex couple married in our home country — does Thailand recognise this?**
Thailand legalised same-sex marriage in 2025. Foreign same-sex marriages can now be registered in Thailand under the same Kor Ror 22 process. **[verify current Amphoe practice — implementation may vary by district]**

**9. What if I lose the legalised document?**
You'll need to restart the process. Make multiple certified copies of the legalised document and keep originals secured. Some agencies retain digital copies as a backup service.

**10. Can my Thai spouse start the process while I'm still abroad?**
The Thai-side steps (translation, MFA, Amphoe) can be initiated by your Thai spouse with your authorisation. Your physical presence is typically required only at the Amphoe registration step.

**11. Do we need to register if we don't plan to stay in Thailand?**
For visa purposes — only if you want a Non-O Marriage visa. For other purposes (joint property, inheritance, joint banking) — yes, registration is recommended. If you only visit Thailand occasionally as tourists, formal registration isn't strictly required.

**12. What if our marriage was registered electronically (online or video ceremony)?**
Some online or video-based marriages (popular in some US states during COVID) face difficulties at Thai authentication stages. The marriage must be formally registered by an in-person government authority in your home country. Online ceremonies that aren't backed by physical government registration may not be recognised.

## 2025–2026 changes

- **Same-sex marriage recognition** (effective 2025): Foreign same-sex marriages now registerable in Thailand under the same Kor Ror 22 process. Amphoe practice may vary by district as implementation rolls out.
- **MFA appointment system since 2025:** Walk-in legalisation no longer accepted; appointments required, wait 2–4 weeks.
- **Stricter document scrutiny:** Foreign marriage certificates that previously passed informally are now more thoroughly checked by Amphoe officers and Immigration.
- **Fee structure change post-27 April 2026:** Affects multiple consular services; verify current fees.
