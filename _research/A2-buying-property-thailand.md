## The fundamental rule: foreigners cannot own land

**Thai law prohibits foreigners from owning land directly.** This is a hard rule with limited exceptions (BOI promotion, ministerial approval). The vast majority of foreign property buyers in Pattaya purchase **condominiums**, where freehold ownership is allowed.

## What foreigners CAN own

### Condominiums (freehold) — the standard path

**Foreigners can own up to 49% of the total sellable floor area** of any registered condominium project in their own name with a freehold title.

- The 49% rule is measured by **floor area**, not unit count
- Once a building reaches 49% foreign ownership, no more units can be sold to foreigners on freehold (until existing foreign owners sell to Thais)
- Foreign quota varies by building — verify before purchase

**This is the cleanest path for most foreign buyers.**

### Buildings (without land)

Foreigners can own buildings (houses, structures) where the land beneath is leased. The structure is yours; the land is leased typically for 30 years (renewable).

### Leasehold (30 years)

Foreigners can lease land or property for up to 30 years (registered leasehold). Renewals possible but not legally guaranteed.

### Workarounds (use with caution)

- **Thai company structure:** Form a Thai Limited Company (51% Thai / 49% foreign) that buys land. **Increasingly scrutinised** in 2025–2026 for nominee shareholding (illegal). Avoid unless properly structured by a Thai lawyer.
- **Marriage to Thai national:** Thai spouse owns land directly; foreign spouse signs away rights at Land Department. Land remains with Thai spouse upon death/divorce unless protected by usufruct or will.
- **Usufruct (right of use):** Foreigner gets lifetime use of land owned by Thai national (often spouse). Doesn't transfer ownership but secures use rights.

## The 49% foreign quota in detail

Every condominium building in Thailand has a foreign ownership quota:

- **Default: 49% of total sellable floor area** can be owned by foreigners on freehold
- Some BOI-promoted buildings or special zones may exceed this
- **Once a building hits its quota:** further sales to foreigners are leasehold only (typically 30-year leases from the building developer)

**Practical reality in Pattaya:**

- Most popular foreign-friendly buildings (View Talay, Centara-managed projects, beachfront premium developments) are at or near 49% saturation
- Newer developments often have available foreign quota
- "Foreign quota for sale" is a common term in Pattaya real estate listings

## The Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FET / FETF)

To register a condo purchase under your foreign name, you must:

1. Transfer purchase funds **from abroad in foreign currency** to a Thai bank
2. Thai bank issues a **Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FET)** documenting the inflow
3. Submit FET to Land Department at title transfer

**Critical points:**

- Funds must arrive as foreign currency, then convert to THB in Thailand
- Cannot use THB you already have in Thailand (must come from abroad)
- Cannot use credit cards or cash — must be a bank wire
- All amounts now require FET (was $50,000+ pre-2018, now any amount)

**Wise / Revolut considerations:** As of mid-2026, Wise's new Thai-based entity rules complicate FET issuance for property purchases. Verify with your bank before assuming Wise transfers will produce a usable FET.

### Off-plan vs resale

**Off-plan (pre-construction):**
- Lower entry prices
- Risk: developer delays, project cancellation, quality variance
- Payment in stages over construction period
- 5-year minimum hold for tax-efficient resale

**Resale:**
- Immediate occupation
- Verifiable building quality
- Higher upfront cost but lower risk
- Most foreign buyers in Pattaya recommend resale for first purchase

## Closing costs — what the brochure won't tell you

Pattaya condo purchase closing costs typically run **5–8% of purchase price**:

| Cost | Typical rate | Who pays |
|---|---|---|
| Transfer fee | 2% of appraised value | Usually split 50/50; negotiable |
| Specific Business Tax (SBT) | 3.3% if seller held <5 years | Seller (sometimes shifted) |
| Stamp Duty | 0.5% of appraised value | If SBT not applicable |
| Withholding Tax | 1% (corporate) or progressive (individual) | Seller |
| Legal fees | 25,000–80,000 THB | Buyer |
| Sinking fund (one-time) | 350–1,000 THB/sqm | Buyer |
| Common area / juristic fees (first year) | 30–80 THB/sqm/month annually | Buyer |

**Practical advice:** Budget **8% of purchase price** above the listed price for closing costs and first-year juristic fees.

## Mortgage as a foreigner

**Limited but possible:**

- **UOB Thailand:** Has products for foreign buyers in Thailand (LTR / Elite preferred)
- **MBK Guarantee, ICBC:** Some products for specific foreign profiles
- **Chinese banks (ICBC):** Aggressive in Thai property lending to Chinese buyers
- **Most Thai banks:** Generally do NOT lend to foreign buyers

**Typical foreign mortgage terms (2026):**

- LTV: 50–70% maximum (much lower than Thai citizens)
- Interest rate: 6–8% (vs 4–6% for Thai citizens)
- Term: 5–15 years (shorter than 25–30 years for Thai citizens)
- Down payment: 30–50% required

**Practical reality:** Most foreign Pattaya condo buyers pay cash. Mortgages are available but punitive in pricing and terms.

## Renting your condo out

**Allowed but regulated:**

- Long-term rental (1+ years): allowed
- Short-term rental (Airbnb-style, under 30 days): **prohibited** in most condo buildings under Thai Hotel Act
- Some condo juristic offices specifically prohibit rentals; others permit

**Income tax on rental:**

- Rental income subject to Thai tax (as Thai-source income)
- Standard withholding by tenant: 5%
- Annual filing required

## Selling — the 5-year rule

**Holding period matters for tax:**

- **<5 years:** Specific Business Tax (SBT) of 3.3% applies on resale value
- **5+ years:** SBT exempted, only Stamp Duty (0.5%) applies

This 5-year rule effectively penalises short-term flipping and rewards long-term holding.

## Common pitfalls

1. **Buying at 49% saturation:** The building has hit foreign quota; you can only get leasehold. Verify free freehold availability before signing.
2. **Wrong company structure:** Thai Limited Company arrangements with nominee shareholders are increasingly raided. Use a properly-structured BOI or genuine Thai partner setup, or stick to condos.
3. **No FET form:** Without proper Foreign Exchange Transaction Form documentation, title cannot be registered in foreign name. Coordinate with Thai bank before sending funds.
4. **Off-plan with weak developer:** Project delays, quality issues, even cancellations have happened. Stick to established developers (Sansiri, AP, Ananda, Origin, Major) for off-plan purchases.
5. **Underestimating juristic fees:** Annual common area fees can be 30,000–80,000+ THB/year on a premium condo. Budget annually.
6. **Inheritance complications:** Foreign heirs may have to dispose of inherited condos within 1 year if they don't qualify for ownership. See A5 will writing guide.
7. **Renting on Airbnb:** Short-term rental is largely illegal in Thai condos. Fines and even building eviction possible.

## When buying makes sense vs renting

**Buying typically makes sense if:**
- Plan to live in Thailand 8+ years
- Have full purchase amount in cash (no mortgage stress)
- Settled on Pattaya as long-term home
- Want to leverage capital appreciation in growing market

**Renting typically makes more sense if:**
- Plan to live in Thailand <5 years
- Want flexibility to test neighbourhoods or move
- Don't want maintenance responsibilities
- Want capital available for other investments

**Honest math:** A 5M THB Pattaya condo with rental yield of ~5% produces ~250k THB/year (~21k THB/month) — but maintenance, juristic, vacancy, and management cut this significantly. Compare to rental cost of 18k–25k THB/month for similar property. Break-even typically 8–12 years assuming modest capital appreciation.

## 10 FAQ questions

**1. Can foreigners really own freehold property in Thailand?**
**Yes — condominium units only**, up to 49% of any building's total foreign ownership. Land cannot be owned directly.

**2. What's the minimum I should budget?**
~3,000,000 THB (~$85,000 USD) for a basic 1-bed condo in non-prime Pattaya area. Premium beachfront starts around 5,000,000 THB.

**3. Closing costs?**
Roughly **8% of purchase price total** including transfer fees, taxes, legal, and first-year juristic fees.

**4. Do I need to be in Thailand to buy?**
Helpful but not required for the entire process. Power of Attorney to a Thai lawyer can complete most steps. Bank account opening typically requires your presence.

**5. What's a "Chanote" title?**
Chanote (Nor Sor 4) is the strongest land title — full ownership document. For condos, the equivalent is the unit ownership certificate. Verify the title type before purchase.

**6. Can I buy land for a house?**
Foreigners cannot own land. Workarounds include 30-year lease, marriage to Thai (with usufruct protection), or Thai company structure (risky in 2026). Standalone houses on land are generally avoided by foreign buyers.

**7. Is Pattaya a good investment market?**
Pattaya prices have appreciated modestly over 5 years (~3–5%/year). Better growth in central Bangkok and some Phuket areas. Pattaya is more about lifestyle than capital gains.

**8. What about new BOI ownership rules?**
BOI-promoted projects sometimes allow foreign majority or special quotas. Check specific project's BOI status. Standard residential condos remain at 49% foreign quota.

**9. Can I include my Thai partner on the title?**
Yes — joint title (you + Thai national) possible. Implications for inheritance, divorce, and tax differ from sole ownership. Consult lawyer.

**10. Should I use a real estate agent?**
**Yes for most foreign buyers** — local Pattaya agents handle quota verification, due diligence, and contract translation. Typical commission: 3–5% of purchase price (often paid by seller).
