## US-specific visa requirements

### FBI Identity History Summary (police clearance)

For Non-O-A retirement, Non-O-X, LTR applications:

- **FBI Identity History Summary** (formerly "FBI Background Check") — federal-level criminal record
- Cost: $18 (electronic) or $35 (paper)
- Time: 3–5 business days online
- Apply: https://www.fbi.gov/services/cjis/identity-history-summary-checks
- **Use FBI-approved Channeler** for fastest service (companies like Accurate Biometrics)

### Document legalisation chain (for foreign marriage, etc.)

US is a Hague Apostille Convention member, but apostilles vary by state:

1. **State-level apostille** — Secretary of State of the state where document was issued (varies dramatically by state: New York 2 days; California 4 weeks; Florida 1–2 weeks)
2. **For federal documents:** US Department of State authentication
3. **Royal Thai Embassy Washington D.C.** or consulate (LA, Chicago, NY) certification
4. **Thai translation** in Pattaya
5. **Thai MFA legalisation** in Bangkok
6. **Amphoe registration** for marriages → Kor Ror 22

**Total US chain:** 6–10 weeks (state apostille is the bottleneck), $100–$200.

### US tax obligations — the major US-specific issue

**Critical:** US is one of only two countries (with Eritrea) practising citizenship-based taxation. **You file US taxes regardless of residency.**

#### Annual obligations for US expats

1. **IRS Form 1040** — annual federal tax return, regardless of where you live
2. **Form 2555 (FEIE)** — Foreign Earned Income Exclusion ($132,900 for tax year 2026)
3. **Form 1116** — Foreign Tax Credit (avoid double taxation)
4. **FBAR (FinCEN 114)** — Report Foreign Bank Account if aggregate foreign accounts >$10,000 at any point during the year
5. **FATCA Form 8938** — Report foreign financial assets if >$200,000 single / $300,000 peak (single filers abroad)

**Penalties for non-compliance:**

- FBAR penalties: $10,000–$100,000 per violation (or up to 50% of account balance)
- FATCA penalties: $10,000+ per violation
- Tax evasion: criminal prosecution

**Streamlined Foreign Offshore Procedures:** IRS amnesty program for non-willful late filers — used by many Americans who discovered they should have been filing.

#### US-Thailand FATCA agreement

US and Thailand signed FATCA Intergovernmental Agreement in 2016. Thai banks **automatically report qualifying US accounts to the IRS**. Don't assume bank account opacity.

#### Social Security in Thailand

- **US-Thailand totalization agreement does NOT exist** (some other US-Asia agreements do)
- US Social Security continues to be paid to US retirees in Thailand — direct deposit available
- US Social Security is **only taxable in the US** under US-Thailand DTA (good news for US retirees)

## US-Thailand Double Tax Agreement

Thailand has a DTA with the US providing:

- **Foreign Tax Credit** for US-paid tax on income also taxable in Thailand
- **Source rules** for various income types
- **Special treatment** for Social Security (US-only taxation)

**Practical implication:** Most US retirees pay US tax first (citizenship-based), then have credits available against any Thai tax on the same income. Net effect: usually no additional Thai tax beyond what's already paid to US.

**Exception:** Income earned post-2024 and remitted to Thailand in same year (Thai 2024 rule change) may face Thai tax that exceeds US tax — generating actual additional tax burden.

## 8 FAQ questions

**1. Do I really still owe US taxes if I live in Thailand?**
**Yes** — US citizenship-based taxation continues regardless of residency. File 1040, FBAR, possibly FATCA Form 8938 annually. Foreign Earned Income Exclusion ($132,900 for 2026) and Foreign Tax Credit reduce or eliminate actual tax owed for most.

**2. Will my Social Security check be deposited in Thailand?**
**Yes** — Social Security Direct Deposit works to most Thai banks. Many US retirees use Bangkok Bank or Kasikorn for SS deposit. Talk to Social Security Administration before relocating.

**3. Can I use Medicare in Thailand?**
**No** — Medicare doesn't cover care outside the US. US retirees in Thailand typically use private insurance (Pacific Cross, Cigna Global, Bangkok Hospital plans, etc.) or pay out of pocket. See [G7](./G7-health-insurance.md).

**4. What's the FBAR threshold?**
$10,000 aggregate value across ALL foreign accounts at ANY point during the year. Very low threshold — most expats with a Thai bank account exceed it. File annually via FinCEN 114.

**5. Should I close my US bank accounts?**
**Generally no** — keep at least one US bank for SS deposit, tax refunds, US-side transactions. Some expat-friendly options: USAA, Schwab International, Charles Schwab Bank.

**6. Do US retirees file Thai tax?**
**Most don't currently**, but the 2024 rule change increases scrutiny. If you remit large amounts of foreign income (>$50k/year) and stay 180+ days, talk to a Thai tax specialist.

**7. Where do most US retirees live in Pattaya?**
Jomtien, Pratumnak primarily. Some in central Pattaya. East Pattaya for budget options. American expat communities concentrate in specific buildings.

**8. What about US driving licence in Thailand?**
Valid with International Driving Permit for 1 year. After that, convert to Thai (505 THB, no driving test). State driving licences are accepted; convert via Banglamung DLT.
