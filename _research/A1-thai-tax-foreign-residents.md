## The 180-day rule — when you become a Thai tax resident

You are a **Thai tax resident** for any calendar year in which you are physically present in Thailand for **180 days or more** (Section 41, Thai Revenue Code).

- **Cumulative**, not consecutive — 100 days in spring + 100 days in autumn = 200 days = tax resident
- **Calendar year** (1 January–31 December) — resets every year
- Counts include days of arrival and departure
- Day-counting matters; use a flight-record spreadsheet to track precisely

**If you spend fewer than 180 days in a calendar year, you are NOT a Thai tax resident.** Most short-stay tourists, METV holders, and split-time digital nomads avoid this status.

## The 2024 tax law change (now fully enforced in 2026)

Until 31 December 2023, Thailand had a major loophole: foreign-source income brought into Thailand in a *later* tax year than it was earned was **not taxable**. Many long-stay expats simply waited until the next year to remit foreign income, paying zero Thai tax.

**This loophole closed on 1 January 2024.**

### New rule (effective 1 January 2024, fully enforced in 2026)

For Thai tax residents (180+ days/year):

- **Foreign-source income is taxable in the year it is remitted to Thailand**, regardless of when it was earned (subject to two exceptions below)
- The previous 1-year-lag exemption is **abolished**
- Includes: pension income, salary from foreign employer, freelance income, investment income, capital gains, rental income from foreign property — all if remitted to Thailand

### Two important exceptions

1. **Pre-2024 income protection:** Income earned before 1 January 2024 is **not taxable** under the new rules even if remitted now. Your savings from before 2024 stay tax-free.
2. **Proposed 2025/2026 carve-outs:** The Thai Revenue Department announced (mid-2025) it is drafting legislation to exempt foreign-source income remitted within two tax years of being earned. **As of April 2026, this proposal has NOT been formally enacted.** Plan based on current rules.

### LTR visa exception (the major workaround)

**LTR-W, LTR-P, and LTR-T holders** are explicitly exempted under Royal Decree 743:
- Foreign-source income is **tax-exempt** when remitted to Thailand for these LTR categories
- Royal Decree 743 specifically excludes them from the 2024 rule change
- This is the single biggest commercial reason to choose LTR over Privilege/Elite for high-income residents

**LTR-H gets a different benefit:** 17% flat income tax rate on Thai-sourced employment income. Foreign-source treatment differs.

## Thai income tax rates (progressive)

For Thai tax residents, taxable income is taxed at progressive rates:

| Annual taxable income (THB) | Rate |
|---|---|
| 0 – 150,000 | 0% |
| 150,001 – 300,000 | 5% |
| 300,001 – 500,000 | 10% |
| 500,001 – 750,000 | 15% |
| 750,001 – 1,000,000 | 20% |
| 1,000,001 – 2,000,000 | 25% |
| 2,000,001 – 5,000,000 | 30% |
| 5,000,001+ | 35% |

**Effective rates** are typically lower than marginal — taxable income is calculated after standard deductions and personal allowances (~150k–250k THB).

**Indicative effective rates** for foreign income remitted at various levels (single resident, no dependents):

- $30k/year remitted (~1.05M THB): effective ~10–12% Thai tax
- $50k/year remitted (~1.75M THB): effective ~15–17%
- $100k/year remitted (~3.5M THB): effective ~22–25%
- $200k/year remitted (~7M THB): effective ~28–30%

## Double Tax Agreements (DTAs)

Thailand has DTAs with **most Western countries**, including: UK, US, Germany, France, Australia, Canada, Italy, Netherlands, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, Switzerland, Russia, India, China, Japan, and many others.

DTAs typically provide:

- **Foreign tax credit** — tax already paid in your home country can offset Thai tax
- **Exemption for specific income types** (e.g., civil service pensions for some countries)
- **Source rules** — which country has primary right to tax specific income
- **Tie-breaker rules** for determining tax residency when both countries claim you

**Critical: DTAs reduce or eliminate double taxation, not Thai tax altogether.** If your home country tax rate is higher than Thai, the credit fully offsets. If Thai is higher, you pay the difference.

## Country-specific notes

### UK

- UK State Pension: classified as foreign-source income, **fully taxable** in Thailand when remitted (no specific exemption under UK-Thailand DTA)
- UK private/company pensions: taxable in Thailand if remitted
- Civil service pensions: typically remain UK-taxable under DTA, exempt in Thailand
- UK State Pension is **frozen** when you move to Thailand (no annual increases)

### US

- US Social Security: taxable under US-Thailand DTA in **US only** (good news for US retirees)
- US private pensions / 401(k) / IRA distributions: more complex; case-by-case under DTA
- FATCA: US citizens must report foreign financial accounts (Form 8938 if assets >$200k single, $300k peak; FBAR if >$10k aggregate)
- US tax filing obligation continues regardless of Thai residency — file 1040 yearly

### Germany

- Civil service pensions: always taxed in Germany, exempt in Thailand under DTA
- State, company, private pensions: taxable in Thailand if remitted (and Thai tax resident)
- German tax filing depends on individual situation — consult specialist

### Australia

- Australian retirement income (super, pension): generally taxable in Australia first, with DTA credit available in Thailand
- Australian tax residency status separately determined — consult Australian tax specialist

### EU general

- Most EU country pensions (state, company, private): taxable in Thailand if remitted, with DTA credit for tax already paid in home country
- Civil service pensions typically retain home-country exclusivity

### Russia

- Russia-Thailand DTA exists
- Russian-source income subject to Thai tax if remitted; Russian tax credits available
- Russian sanctions and banking complications affect remittance practicalities (separate issue)

### India

- India-Thailand DTA exists
- Indian-source income (salary, business, investment) potentially taxable in Thailand if remitted
- Consult Indian + Thai tax specialist for complex cases

## How to file Thai tax

### Tax Identification Number (TIN)

- Required if you become a Thai tax resident with assessable income
- Apply at Thai Revenue Department office (Pattaya has a regional office in Banglamung)
- Free to obtain
- One-time issuance

### Annual filing

- **Tax year:** 1 January–31 December (calendar year)
- **Filing deadline:** 31 March of following year (March 31, 2027 for 2026 tax year)
- **Form:** PND 90 or 91 depending on income type
- **Online filing:** Thai Revenue Department e-filing system (Thai language; English limited)
- **In person:** At Revenue Department offices

### What gets reported

For Thai tax residents:

- All foreign-source income remitted to Thailand during the tax year (2024+ income only)
- Thai-source income (rental, employment, etc.)
- Capital gains realised in Thailand
- Pre-existing Thai tax obligations

**For Thai non-residents (under 180 days):** only Thai-source income.

## Practical compliance — what most expats actually do

Reality check: Thai tax compliance for foreign expats has **historically been weak** — many long-stay foreigners simply didn't file. The 2024 rule change is increasing scrutiny but enforcement remains uneven.

**Compliance tiers in 2026:**

1. **Strict compliance:** File Thai tax returns yearly, declare all remitted foreign income, claim DTA credits. Recommended for high-net-worth individuals, LTR holders, those with significant remittances.
2. **Partial compliance:** File for Thai-source income only; quietly under-declare foreign remittances. Common but risky as scrutiny increases.
3. **Non-compliance:** Don't file at all. Common historically; increasingly risky.

**Honest professional advice:** Move toward strict compliance, especially if:
- You remit large amounts (>$50k/year)
- You hold an LTR visa (claiming exemption requires filing)
- You have a Thai bank account with substantial flows
- You're applying for LTR or Thai residency

## Property purchase tax implications

When buying Thai property as a foreigner:

- **Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FET):** Required — proves source of foreign funds for the purchase. Thai bank issues at time of inward transfer.
- **Purchase taxes:** Transfer fee (2% of appraised value), Specific Business Tax (if held <5 years), Stamp Duty, Withholding Tax — typically 5–8% of purchase price total
- **Annual property tax:** Land and Building Tax — generally low for personal residence (under 1% of value)

## 12 FAQ questions

**1. Do I have to pay Thai tax if I retire in Pattaya?**
If you spend 180+ days/year in Thailand AND you remit foreign income to Thailand, **yes** (with DTA offsets). If you stay under 180 days OR only remit pre-2024 savings, **no**.

**2. What about my home country pension — is it double-taxed?**
**No** — DTAs prevent true double taxation. You pay the higher of the two countries' rates on each type of income, with credits ensuring you don't pay both fully. Consult a tax specialist for your specific country.

**3. The LTR visa really exempts foreign income from Thai tax?**
Yes — for LTR-W, LTR-P, and LTR-T categories under Royal Decree 743. This is a substantial benefit. LTR-H gets a different benefit (17% flat rate on Thai-source income).

**4. What if I live on pre-2024 savings?**
**Not taxable** — the 2024 rule applies to income earned 1 Jan 2024 onwards. Pre-2024 savings remitted now are exempt.

**5. Do I have to remit to be taxable?**
**Yes — only remitted income is taxable.** Money kept in your home country and never sent to Thailand is not taxable in Thailand (regardless of when earned).

**6. How does Thai Revenue track my remittances?**
Thai banks report foreign-currency inflows above certain thresholds. The CRS (Common Reporting Standard) and FATCA share account information across borders. Don't assume your remittances are invisible.

**7. What about crypto withdrawals to Thailand?**
**Treated as foreign-source income.** Same rules apply — taxable when remitted if earned post-2024 and you're a tax resident. Crypto exchange withdrawals to Thai bank accounts are increasingly scrutinised.

**8. Is there inheritance tax in Thailand?**
**Yes** — but only on estates exceeding 100 million THB. Below that threshold, no Thai inheritance tax. See `/adjacent/will-writing-thailand/` for estate planning.

**9. Should I get a Thai TIN even if I'm not sure I'll file?**
**Yes if you're a long-term resident.** TIN is one-time, free, and useful for many administrative purposes. Doesn't trigger filing obligation by itself.

**10. What's the penalty for not filing?**
Late filing: 2% per month interest + surcharges. Wilful non-disclosure: criminal penalties possible but rarely enforced for foreign-source income historically. Risk is increasing.

**11. Can I be a "tax resident of nowhere"?**
Most countries' tax laws prevent this. You'll be tax resident somewhere. The question is whether you're tax resident in Thailand, your home country, or both (and DTA tie-breaker rules apply).

**12. Should I get professional advice?**
**Yes if:** annual remittances >$30k/year, LTR holder, complex international income, significant Thai property, business interests. The cost (5,000–25,000 THB/year) typically pays for itself many times over.
