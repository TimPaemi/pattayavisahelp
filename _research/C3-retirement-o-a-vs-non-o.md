## At a glance

| | Non-O-A | Non-O (in-country) |
|---|---|---|
| **Where applied** | Thai Embassy abroad | In Thailand (after arrival) |
| **Initial visa duration** | 1 year (multiple entry possible) | 90 days (single) |
| **Bank deposit needed** | 800,000 THB or 65k/month income | 800,000 THB or 65k/month income |
| **Seasoning rule** | Pre-application (varies by embassy) | 2 months at Jomtien |
| **Health insurance** | **Mandatory (40k OPD / 400k IPD)** | **Not required by law** |
| **Police clearance** | Required (home country) | Not required |
| **Medical certificate** | Required | Not required |
| **Annual extension** | Yes — at Jomtien | Yes — at Jomtien |
| **Cost (initial)** | 2,000 THB embassy fee | 2,000 THB conversion (TM.86/87) |
| **Cost (annual)** | 1,900 THB extension | 1,900 THB extension |
| **Total time to in-hand visa** | 6–10 weeks (police clearance bottleneck) | ~3 months (2-month seasoning + 15-day Non-O processing) |

## The core difference

**One factor decides 90% of cases: health insurance.**

- **Non-O-A requires it.** 40k THB OPD + 400k THB IPD minimum, valid for full visa period, on approved provider list. Premiums for over-65s: 50k–120k THB/year.
- **Non-O in-country doesn't require it.** Some immigration offices encourage it; none currently mandate it as of April 2026.

For a 70-year-old retiree facing 80k+ THB annual insurance premiums, the in-country Non-O path saves substantial money each year — potentially the difference between affordable retirement in Thailand or not.

## Why some retirees still choose O-A

Despite the insurance burden, O-A has real advantages:

1. **1-year visa from day one.** Non-O starts at 90 days, then extends to 1 year — more steps.
2. **Can be applied from home before relocating.** Less stressful for first-time retirees.
3. **Multi-entry option at some embassies.** Useful for trips home before the 1-year extension.
4. **Embassy paperwork done outside Thailand.** Easier for some retirees who prefer dealing with their own country's bureaucracy.
5. **No need to convert from tourist** at Jomtien (which adds 2,000 THB and processing time).
6. **Cleaner documentation trail** for retirees who travel frequently or have complex residency situations.

## Why most retirees choose Non-O in-country

Non-O is the more popular path in Pattaya specifically:

1. **No mandatory health insurance** — biggest single saving for older retirees
2. **No police clearance** — saves 4–8 weeks of waiting on FBI / ACPO / ACIC checks
3. **No medical certificate** — saves a doctor's visit and notarisation
4. **Simpler embassy step** — 90-day Non-O is easier to get than 1-year O-A
5. **Familiar Pattaya process** — Jomtien staff handle Non-O extensions every day; many local agents specialise in this exact flow
6. **More flexibility in arrival timing** — arrive on tourist visa, decide later

**Honest summary:** Non-O is the practical choice for the majority of Pattaya retirees, especially those over 65 facing high insurance premiums.

## Who O-A is right for

- **Under 65, healthy** — insurance is affordable
- **Want 1-year visa locked in before relocating**
- **Prefer doing paperwork in home country** before moving
- **Have clean police record + accessible national-level clearance** (FBI, ACPO, ACIC)
- **Value the multi-entry option some embassies offer**

**Persona:** Mark, 55, recently retired British engineer, in good health. Wants to apply at the Royal Thai Embassy London before flying to Pattaya. O-A makes sense.

## Who Non-O in-country is right for

- **Over 65, especially with pre-existing conditions** that make insurance expensive
- **Can't afford 50k+ THB/year insurance**
- **Already in Thailand on tourist or visa-exempt entry**
- **Want to "test" Pattaya before committing to relocation**
- **Plans to stay continuously without home-country trips initially**
- **Prefer in-Thailand support from Pattaya visa agents**

**Persona:** John, 72, US retiree with diabetes. Insurance for O-A would cost 100k+ THB/year. Non-O in-country path saves him this cost annually. He'll arrive on tourist visa, convert at Jomtien, season the 800k THB, then extend.

## Decision tree

```
Are you healthy enough for affordable health insurance (typically under 65)?
├── YES → Do you want the visa before flying to Thailand?
│         ├── YES → Non-O-A
│         └── NO → Non-O in-country (still simpler in-Thailand)
│
└── NO (older or pre-existing conditions) → Non-O in-country (no insurance required)
```

## Same destination, different paths

Both paths end up at the same place: a 1-year Extension of Stay stamp at Jomtien Immigration, renewable annually. The 800k THB seasoning rules, 90-day reporting, re-entry permit needs, and TM30 obligations are identical from year 2 onwards.

**Year 1 differences disappear from year 2.** The choice is purely about how you start.

## Hidden tradeoffs

### O-A's "year 2 insurance gotcha"

Some O-A holders assume insurance is a one-time requirement. **It's not.** Insurance must be maintained continuously throughout each annual extension. Letting coverage lapse can trigger extension refusal.

### Non-O's "documentation surprise"

Non-O in-country path's lower paperwork burden surprises some retirees who assumed Thai bureaucracy meant lots of forms. The reality at Jomtien is straightforward: TM.7 + bank book + bank letter + TM30 + photos. Done.

### The "switching" question

Can you switch from O-A to Non-O later? **In principle yes.** Most Pattaya retirees who started on O-A and later struggle with insurance simply let the O-A lapse and re-enter on tourist visa, then convert to Non-O in-country at Jomtien. Cleaner than trying to formally "downgrade."

### Embassy-by-embassy O-A variance

Some embassies (UK, US, Australia) are stricter on O-A documentation. Others (Vientiane Laos, Penang Malaysia) historically more lenient — but **the embassy you apply at must match your nationality or legal residence**, so this isn't a free-choice optimization.

## 8 FAQ questions

**1. If I'm under 65 and healthy, which is better?**
Either works. **O-A** if you want the visa before relocating. **Non-O in-country** if you're already in Thailand or want to skip the police clearance + medical paperwork.

**2. If I'm 70+ with a pre-existing condition, which is better?**
**Non-O in-country.** No mandatory insurance is the deciding factor — annual savings of 80k–150k THB.

**3. Are the financial requirements really the same?**
**Yes.** Both require 800,000 THB Thai bank deposit (or 65,000 THB/month income). The deposit, seasoning, and ongoing rules are identical.

**4. Does the Jomtien process differ between O-A and Non-O extensions?**
At extension time (year 2 onwards), they're functionally identical. Year 1 differs because O-A has insurance documentation in the file already.

**5. Can I get a re-entry permit on either?**
**Yes** — both require re-entry permits before exiting Thailand. Same fees (1,000 THB single, 3,800 THB multiple). See [G3 Re-entry permits guide](./G3-re-entry-permits.md).

**6. What about UK/US/Australian retirees who can't get embassy income letters?**
Doesn't matter for either path — both can use the **800,000 THB deposit method** instead of income proof. The embassy income letter route is only needed for the income method.

**7. Which is more popular in Pattaya?**
**Non-O in-country**, by a substantial margin. Health insurance economics drive this — most Pattaya retirees prefer the in-country path.

**8. Will the rules change in 2026?**
The Non-O in-country **insurance requirement could be added in future** — it's been periodically rumoured but not enacted. As of April 2026, the in-country path remains insurance-free. Monitor for changes.
