## Why expats need a Thai will

**Thai law applies to all assets located in Thailand**, regardless of the owner's nationality. This means:

- Your home-country will alone is not sufficient if you have Thai assets
- Without a Thai will, Thai assets pass under Thai intestate succession rules
- Foreign heirs face additional complications (especially with Thai condos)
- Estate process in Thailand can take 12+ months without proper planning

**The standard recommendation:** Have **two wills** — one in your home country (for foreign assets) and one in Thailand (for Thai assets), each with a clause limiting its scope to prevent accidentally revoking each other.

## What goes in a Thai will

Typical Thai assets that should be covered:

- **Thai bank accounts** (any balance)
- **Thai condominium ownership** (unit + percentage in juristic body)
- **Thai company shares** (if you own a Thai company)
- **Thai vehicle ownership**
- **Furniture, personal effects** in Thailand
- **Thai life insurance policies**
- **Thai-based investments** (if any)
- **Lease rights** (if you hold registered leasehold property)

## Thai inheritance law basics

### Without a will (intestate succession)

Thai Civil Code defines succession order:

1. **Descendants** (children, grandchildren) — first
2. **Parents** — equal share with descendants
3. **Brothers/sisters of full blood** — next
4. **Brothers/sisters of half-blood** — next
5. **Grandparents** — next
6. **Uncles/aunts** — last

**Spouse special rule:** Surviving spouse inherits alongside whichever class is currently entitled. Share depends on class involved.

**For foreigners with no Thai relatives:** Without a will, Thai assets become subject to lengthy court process to identify heirs and possibly revert to the state if no eligible heirs identified.

### With a Thai will

You direct exactly who inherits what. Process is faster and more predictable.

## Condo inheritance complications

The Thai Condominium Act has specific rules for foreign inheritance:

- **Foreign heirs of a Thai condo must qualify under Section 19** (essentially the same eligibility as foreign condo buyers)
- **If the foreign heir doesn't qualify** (e.g., the building has hit its 49% foreign quota and the heir would push it over), they have **1 year to dispose of the unit**
- After 1 year, the Director-General of the Land Department can dispose of the unit and retain a 5% fee

**Practical implication:** When leaving a Thai condo to a foreign heir, ensure the building has foreign quota available, or build instructions in your will for sale and proceeds distribution.

## Inheritance tax

**Thailand has inheritance tax**, but with a high threshold:

- **Tax-free up to 100,000,000 THB (~$2.8M USD)** of assets
- Above that threshold: 5% (immediate family) or 10% (others)
- Most expat estates fall well below the threshold and pay zero inheritance tax

**This makes Thailand favorable for estate planning** for expats with multi-million-dollar estates compared to home countries with lower thresholds (US estate tax exemption is much higher; UK is ~£325k; many EU countries lower).

## Types of Thai wills

Thai law recognises several will formats. Most common for foreigners:

### 1. Holographic will (handwritten)

- Entirely written, dated, and signed by the testator in their own hand
- No witnesses required
- Simplest format but easy to challenge
- Suitable for very simple estates only

### 2. Will witnessed by 2 witnesses

- Most common format
- Written or typed
- Signed by testator in front of two witnesses
- Witnesses sign confirming they observed
- Witnesses cannot be beneficiaries

### 3. Will made before public officer (most secure)

- Drafted/witnessed at Amphoe (district office)
- Officer signs as witness
- Stored in public record
- Hardest to challenge

### 4. Closed will (sealed)

- Written, signed, sealed, then registered at Amphoe
- Contents confidential until death
- Less common but available

**Recommended for most foreigners:** **Witnessed will (Type 2)** prepared by a Thai lawyer specialising in expat estate planning. Best balance of clarity, low cost, and enforceability.

## Drafting a Thai will

### What to include

1. **Personal identification:** Full name, passport number, nationality, address in Thailand
2. **Statement of capacity:** Confirmation you are of sound mind
3. **Revocation clause:** Revokes any prior Thai wills (NOT home-country wills)
4. **Asset inventory:** List of Thai assets (specific accounts, condo unit, vehicles, etc.)
5. **Beneficiary details:** Full names, identification, relationship
6. **Specific gifts vs residual:** Specific assets to specific people; everything else as "residual"
7. **Executor:** Person responsible for administering the estate (typically your lawyer or trusted Thai friend)
8. **Witnesses:** Names, identification, signatures (cannot be beneficiaries)
9. **Date and signature**

### Common clauses

- **Limit-of-scope clause:** "This will pertains only to my assets located in Thailand. Assets located outside Thailand are governed by my will dated [date] in [country]."
- **Foreign-heir condo handling:** Specific instructions if a foreign heir cannot legally hold a Thai condo
- **Charity bequest:** Optional; some expats leave portions to Thai charities

### Where to draft

- **Thai lawyer** specialising in expat estate planning — typical fee 8,000–25,000 THB for standard will
- **DIY templates** available online but high risk of legal weakness
- **Amphoe-prepared:** lower-cost option for very simple estates

## Process after death

### When the testator dies

1. **Family/executor reports death** to Thai authorities (death certificate issued)
2. **Will is presented** at Thai court (Court of First Instance)
3. **Probate hearing** — court verifies will validity (1–6 months typical)
4. **Executor administers estate** under court supervision
5. **Assets distributed** per will instructions
6. **Final accounting** to court closes the estate

**Total time:** 6–18 months for most estates. With proper Thai will: faster end. Without will or with disputes: longer.

### Foreign-heir specific steps

For foreign heirs inheriting Thai property:

- Heir must qualify under Thai law (especially condo Section 19)
- If heir is not in Thailand, may need legal representative
- Currency repatriation of inheritance proceeds typically requires FET form
- Tax implications in heir's home country (separate from Thai tax)

## Common pitfalls

1. **Single will only:** Relying on home-country will for Thai assets leaves Thai assets to long Thai court process. Always have both.
2. **Mutual revocation:** Wills accidentally revoking each other because the limit-of-scope clause is missing.
3. **Witness who is beneficiary:** Witnesses cannot be beneficiaries — invalidates their share.
4. **Outdated will:** Marriage, divorce, new property — all require will updates. Review every 3–5 years or after major life events.
5. **Foreign heir cannot inherit Thai condo:** Without contingency plan in will, year-long forced disposal scenario.
6. **Executor in wrong country:** Executor in home country struggles with Thai court process. Use Thai-resident executor for Thai will.
7. **Original lost:** Will only enforceable from original or properly registered copy. Store securely with copy at Amphoe (if registered) or with lawyer.

## 8 FAQ questions

**1. Do I really need a separate Thai will?**
**Yes** if you have Thai assets (bank account, condo, vehicle, etc.). Without it, Thai assets go through complex Thai intestate succession.

**2. How much does a Thai will cost?**
Typical standard will via lawyer: **8,000–25,000 THB**. Complex estates with multiple beneficiaries, conditions, or trusts: 30,000–100,000 THB.

**3. Can a foreign will work in Thailand?**
A valid foreign will is enforceable in Thailand if properly translated, legalised, and certified. **However, a separate Thai will is recommended** to avoid delays and complications, especially for property.

**4. What about my Thai spouse?**
Without a will, Thai spouse inherits under specific rules (alongside other heirs). With a will, you can leave more (or less) to your spouse. Marriage contract considerations also apply.

**5. Inheritance tax — really?**
**Yes, but only on estates >100M THB**. Below that, no Thai inheritance tax. Most expat estates fall below.

**6. What if I have a Thai company?**
Thai company shares can be inherited like other assets, but operational continuity requires planning. Discuss with both your Thai lawyer and accountant.

**7. Can my will specify cremation/burial in Thailand?**
**Yes** — wills commonly include funeral instructions. Some Pattaya expats specify cremation in Thailand to avoid repatriation costs.

**8. Should I use a UK / US / Australian will template adapted for Thailand?**
**Strongly discouraged.** Thai law has specific requirements that template wills may not meet. Use a Thai lawyer who specialises in expat wills.
