## Why this matters

Many foreigners considering Thailand long-term need a Thai company:

- To **hire themselves** (Non-B + work permit, requires Thai company sponsor)
- To **run a Thai business** (restaurant, agency, retail, services)
- To **own land via the Thai company structure** (legal but increasingly scrutinised)
- To **import / export** (Thai legal entity required for some categories)
- To **invoice Thai clients** as a freelancer (cleaner than direct billing)

**Reality check:** For most freelance digital nomads earning foreign income, a Thai company is **not needed** — the DTV visa is the proper structure. Setting up a Thai company is a serious step with annual compliance burden.

## Two main paths

### Path 1: Thai Limited Company (49% foreign / 51% Thai shareholders)

The standard structure. Foreign individual owns up to 49% of shares; Thai partners or shareholders hold 51%+.

- **Cheapest setup**
- **Most flexible** for general business
- **Requires Thai partners** (real partners, not nominees — see Pitfalls)

### Path 2: BOI-promoted company (potentially 100% foreign-owned)

Companies that qualify for BOI (Board of Investment) promotion can have **majority or 100% foreign ownership**.

- Requires the business to fit BOI-targeted industries (15 sectors: tech, manufacturing, EEC priorities, etc.)
- Substantial benefits: tax holidays, import duty exemptions, expedited visas/work permits, EEC location bonuses
- More complex application process (4–8 weeks for approval)
- Costs more upfront (legal fees 80,000–200,000 THB) but significant ongoing savings

## Capital requirements

### For private limited companies generally

- **Minimum registered capital:** 50,000 THB (default)
- This is largely a paper number; actual paid-up capital can be lower

### For companies sponsoring foreign work permits

- **2,000,000 THB registered capital per foreign employee** (not optional)
- **Reduced to 1,000,000 THB** if the foreign employee is married to a Thai national
- Capital must be genuinely paid in (not just declared)

### New 2026 requirement: bank statement evidence

Effective 2026, Thai shareholders in 49:51 structures must provide:

- **3 months of bank statements** showing they have the funds
- **Withdrawal/transfer matching** the share payment in amount and date
- This rule is designed to crack down on **nominee shareholding** (illegal practice of using Thai "shareholders" who don't actually hold the funds)

## The 4:1 Thai-to-foreign employee ratio

For non-BOI companies sponsoring foreign work permits:

- **4 Thai employees per 1 foreign employee** (must be on social security with genuine contributions)
- Reduced to 2:1 if the foreign employee is married to a Thai national
- BOI-promoted companies are exempt from this ratio

**This rule kills many small-business expat plans.** A solo freelancer setting up a Thai company to hire themselves needs to find 4 Thai employees first — hard to do for most niche businesses.

## Step-by-step setup process

### 1. Reserve company name (1–2 days)

- Submit 3 proposed names to Department of Business Development (DBD)
- Names approved or rejected; reserve the approved one

### 2. File Memorandum of Association (1–3 days)

- Lists shareholders, capital, business objectives
- Submitted to DBD

### 3. Statutory meeting (1 day)

- Shareholders meet, approve articles of association
- Appoint directors and auditor

### 4. Register company at DBD (1–3 days)

- Submit final documents
- Pay registration fee (5,000–15,000 THB depending on capital)
- Receive Certificate of Incorporation and Tax ID number

### 5. VAT registration (within 30 days if revenue >1.8M THB/year)

- Register at Revenue Department
- Receive VAT number (PP 20)

### 6. Social security registration (within 30 days of hiring first employee)

- Register at Social Security Office
- Begin monthly contributions for Thai employees

### 7. Open Thai bank account in company name

- After Certificate of Incorporation
- Most banks require company directors' personal account at same bank first
- Bank account is needed for capital injection

### 8. WP.32 work permit pre-approval (for foreign hires)

- Department of Employment process
- 7–15 working days
- Must be obtained BEFORE foreign employee applies for Non-B visa

### Total setup time: 2–4 weeks for standard 49:51 company; 6–10 weeks for BOI-promoted

| Item | Cost (THB) |
|---|---|
| Company name reservation | Free |
| DBD registration fee | 5,000–15,000 |
| Legal fees (lawyer drafting) | 25,000–80,000 |
| Notary services | 2,000–5,000 |

| Social Security registration | Free |

| Item | Annual cost (THB) |

## Foreign Business Act — restricted occupations and businesses

Thailand's Foreign Business Act lists **49 categories restricted to Thai majority**:

- **List 1:** Strictly prohibited for foreigners (e.g. newspaper, broadcasting, rice farming)
- **List 2:** Permitted with specific approval (e.g. domestic transport, mineral mining)
- **List 3:** Permitted with Foreign Business License (e.g. retail, services, restaurants)

**Many expat-typical businesses fall in List 3** — restaurants, retail, agencies. Operating in List 3 typically requires either:
- Thai majority shareholder (49:51 structure), OR
- Foreign Business License (FBL) approval, OR
- BOI promotion overriding the restriction

## Common pitfalls

1. **Nominee shareholders (illegal):** Using Thai "shareholders" who don't actually pay for shares is illegal nominee structure. Increasingly raided in 2025–2026. Use real partners or BOI route.
2. **Not meeting 4:1 ratio for work permit:** Setting up a company to hire yourself but only having 1–2 Thai employees fails. Plan staffing realistically.
3. **Underestimating ongoing costs:** Annual audits + bookkeeping + social security can easily exceed 100k THB/year. Budget realistically.
4. **Wrong business category:** Some businesses fall under restricted categories that prevent foreign majority ownership. Verify before starting.
5. **Capital inflation tricks:** Declaring 5M THB capital but only paying in 50k is illegal. The 2026 bank statement requirement makes this much harder.
6. **Forgetting BOI as alternative:** Many expats default to 49:51 when BOI-promoted structure would be much better (100% foreign ownership, tax holidays, no 4:1 ratio). Check BOI eligibility first.
7. **Missing the EEC angle:** Pattaya businesses in qualifying sectors can leverage EEC bonuses on top of standard BOI promotion. Significant benefit if you qualify.

## Pattaya/EEC specific considerations

**EEC (Eastern Economic Corridor):** Includes Chonburi (Pattaya), Rayong, Chachoengsao. Companies operating in EEC-promoted sectors get:

- Enhanced BOI tax holidays (sometimes 13–15 years vs standard 8)
- Land ownership rights for promoted activities
- Streamlined work permit processing at OSOS (Bangkok)
- EEC Labour Administration Center in Chonburi for visa/work permit handling
- Eligible activities include: smart electronics, automotive, robotics, biofuels, medical hub, digital economy, aviation/logistics

**For Pattaya-area entrepreneurs in qualifying sectors, EEC + BOI combo is the gold standard.**

## When NOT to set up a Thai company

Thai company isn't right for everyone:

- **Foreign-income freelancers / digital nomads:** DTV visa is cleaner, no Thai company needed
- **Retirees:** Non-O retirement is the right path; no business activity allowed anyway
- **Short-term ventures:** Setup cost + annual compliance only makes sense for sustained operations
- **One-off projects:** Subcontracting through an existing Thai company often easier

## 10 FAQ questions

**1. Can I own 100% of a Thai company?**
**Only if BOI-promoted** or under specific exemptions. Standard limited companies are 49% maximum foreign ownership.

**2. How much does setup cost?**
Standard 49:51: 30,000–120,000 THB. BOI-promoted: 80,000–200,000+ THB. Plus ongoing 70,000–250,000+ THB/year.

**3. Can I be the sole director?**
Yes — director and shareholder roles are separate. You can be sole director (running the company) while still being minority shareholder (49%). Common structure.

**4. Can my Thai spouse be the 51% shareholder?**
**Yes** — and this reduces work permit ratio from 4:1 to 2:1 and capital requirement from 2M to 1M THB per foreign work permit. Significant benefit for foreign-Thai couples.

**5. What's the cheapest legitimate way?**
Standard 49:51 with real Thai partners (family members, business partners), 50,000 THB declared capital, basic services business. Total first-year cost ~50–80k THB. But this won't support a foreign work permit.

**6. Do I need a physical office?**
For DBD registration: yes (registered address required). Many use virtual office services (5k–20k THB/year). Larger operations or some BOI promotions may require physical office.

**7. How are corporate taxes structured?**
Thai corporate income tax: 20% on profits >300k THB; lower rates for SMEs. VAT: 7% on goods/services (standard rate). Annual filing mandatory.

**8. Can I close the company easily if it fails?**
Closing requires DBD deregistration, Revenue Department clearance, Social Security cancellation, audit. Takes 3–6 months and costs 30k–80k THB. Plan for this from the start.

**9. What about a Sole Proprietorship?**
Thai law generally requires foreigners to operate via a registered limited company, not as a sole proprietor. Limited exceptions exist for specific activities.

**10. Should I use a lawyer or DIY?**
**Use a lawyer.** Thai company law is complex and the consequences of mistakes (illegal nominee structures, wrong business category, etc.) are serious. Legal fees of 25,000–80,000 THB are cheap insurance.
