## At a glance

- **Visa-exempt entry:** 60 days for German passport holders
- **Germans in Pattaya:** ~7,000–8,000 (out of ~30,000 nationally)
- **Most popular long-stay visa:** Non-O retirement (over-50s); LTR or DTV for working/remote
- **Major German-specific advantage:** Eligible for Non-O-X (10-year retirement) — one of only 14 nationalities

## German-specific visa requirements

### Police clearance (Führungszeugnis)

For Non-O-A, Non-O-X, LTR applications:

- **Führungszeugnis** (Certificate of Good Conduct) — issued by Bundesamt für Justiz
- For international use: **Europäisches Führungszeugnis** (European version)
- Cost: ~€13 (national) or ~€17 (international)
- Time: 1–3 weeks
- Apply at local Bürgeramt or online via Federal Office of Justice

### Document legalisation chain (Hague Convention member)

Germany is a Hague Apostille Convention member:

1. **Apostille** from relevant German state authority (varies by Bundesland — Standesamt, Regierungspräsidium, etc.)
2. **Royal Thai Embassy Berlin** or consulates (Frankfurt, Munich) certification
3. **Thai translation** in Pattaya (German→Thai requires specialist; 1,000–2,500 THB)
4. **Thai MFA legalisation** in Bangkok
5. **Amphoe registration** for marriages → Kor Ror 22

**Total German chain:** 5–8 weeks, ~€100–200.

### Non-O-X eligibility — Germany is on the 14-country list

Germany is one of 14 nationalities eligible for the **Non-O-X 10-year retirement visa**. This is a significant advantage over countries like Russia or India that aren't eligible.

For wealthy German retirees (3M+ THB available, 50+, with appropriate insurance), Non-O-X offers:
- 10 years' permission (5+5 structure)
- Reduced bureaucracy vs annual O-A renewal
- See [`/visas/retirement-o-x/`](../visas/retirement-o-x/)

## German tax considerations

### Pension taxation

Germany has detailed DTA with Thailand:

- **Civil service pensions (Beamtenpensionen):** Always taxed in Germany, **exempt in Thailand** under DTA
- **Statutory pensions (gesetzliche Rente):** Taxable in Thailand if you're tax resident and remit funds; German tax credit may apply
- **Company pensions (Betriebsrente):** Taxable in Thailand if tax resident and remitted
- **Private pensions (private Altersvorsorge / Rürup-Rente):** Taxable in Thailand if tax resident and remitted

**German residency status separately determined** — extended Thailand stays may affect German tax residency, with implications for German taxation of all worldwide income.

### Thai tax residency

Same 180-day rule applies — see [A1 Thai tax guide](./A1-thai-tax-foreign-residents.md).

### German fiscal compliance considerations

- Deutschland-Bonn link to Pattaya remittance: large transfers may trigger Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht (BaFin) reporting requirements
- Anti-money laundering rules apply; bank notes documentation
- Selbstanzeige (voluntary disclosure) options if past compliance was incomplete

## 8 FAQ questions

**1. Can I get a Non-O-X 10-year retirement visa as a German?**
**Yes** — Germany is one of 14 eligible nationalities. Requires 3M THB Thai bank deposit and qualifying insurance. See [Non-O-X page](../visas/retirement-o-x/).

**2. What happens to my German pension in Thailand?**
- Beamtenpensionen: taxable in Germany, exempt in Thailand
- Other pensions: taxable in Thailand if remitted and you're tax resident; German tax credit available
- See [A1 Thai tax](./A1-thai-tax-foreign-residents.md)

**3. Can I keep my German Krankenversicherung (health insurance) abroad?**
- **Public Krankenversicherung (GKV):** generally suspended when you move abroad permanently; not portable to Thailand
- **Private Krankenversicherung (PKV):** Some PKV policies maintain coverage in Thailand for limited duration; verify with your insurer
- Most German retirees take Thai or international expat insurance

**4. Do I need to deregister (Abmeldung) from Germany?**
Required if relocating permanently. Consequences: loss of Anmeldung benefits, possible shift in tax residency, ID changes. Discuss with German tax advisor before deregistering.

**5. What's the German community like in Pattaya?**
Active and well-organized — regular meet-ups, social clubs, German-language services (church, school, social events). Largest German expat community outside Germany after Mallorca.

**6. Can I get a German driving licence converted in Thailand?**
**Yes** — Hague convention member; conversion process at Banglamung DLT skips driving test. Translation needed; ~3,000–5,500 THB total. See [A4](./A4-driving-licence-thailand.md).

**7. Where do most Germans live in Pattaya?**
**Jomtien** is the German hub — entire neighbourhoods feel German. Pratumnak for premium. Wong Amat for quieter premium.

**8. What's a Lebensbescheinigung and do I need one?**
"Proof of life" certificate required by some German pension authorities to continue payments to overseas retirees. Issued by German Embassy Bangkok or honorary consulates; typically annual.
