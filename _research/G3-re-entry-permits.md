## Why they exist

A **re-entry permit** is what protects your existing visa or extension of stay when you leave Thailand. If you leave the country while holding a single-entry visa or an in-country extension and do not get a re-entry permit first, **your visa/extension is cancelled the moment you depart**.

When you return, Immigration will admit you on whatever you qualify for at the border (often just a 60-day visa-exempt entry) — you lose your 1-year retirement/marriage/business/ED extension and must start over. (thaiembassy+2, youtube)

**Re-entry permits do not extend your stay or change your visa**; they simply preserve the visa/extension you already have when you exit and re-enter. (rlcoutsourcing+1)

## Single vs multiple re-entry permits

There are two types of Thai re-entry permits:

| Type | Fee | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Single re-entry permit | **1,000 THB** | Allows you to leave and re-enter once while keeping your existing visa/extension valid (cmies.eng.cmu+1) |
| Multiple re-entry permit | **3,800 THB** | Allows you to leave and re-enter unlimited times for as long as your current visa/extension remains valid (cmies.eng.cmu+1) |

- Both types are tied to the underlying permission to stay (your visa or extension). **Once that expires, the re-entry permit is worthless.** (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
- You can buy a single now and later upgrade with another single if you travel again, or buy a multiple upfront if you expect multiple trips. (thaiembassy+1)

**Math note:** Multiple permit pays off after **4 trips** — if you'll exit Thailand 4 or more times during your current extension, the multiple is cheaper than 4 singles.

## Who actually needs a re-entry permit in 2026?

**You need a re-entry permit if you:**

- Hold a 1-year extension of stay (retirement, marriage, business/Non-B, ED, media, Non-O-X, etc.) and want to leave Thailand then return before the extension expires (chiangmaivisarun+1)
- Hold a 90-day Non-Immigrant visa (Non-O, Non-B, ED, etc.) that has been extended in-country, and want to leave and come back on the same permission (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
- Hold a single-entry TR tourist visa that has been extended in Thailand and want to leave and come back on that extended stay (less common) (chiangmaivisarun+1)

**You generally do NOT need a re-entry permit if you:**

- Are in Thailand on a visa-exempt entry only (60-day stamp) — there is no visa/extension to preserve; leaving simply ends that stay (thaiembassy+1)
- Are leaving Thailand and expect to get a completely new visa at a Thai Embassy before returning — you plan to return on that new visa, not your current stay (thaiembassy)
- Hold a long-term visa that is **multiple-entry by design**, such as:
  - Thailand Privilege (Elite) packages
  - LTR Visa (all categories) (ltr.boi+1)
  - SMART Visa (siam-legal+1)
  - METV (Multiple Entry Tourist Visa) (thaiembassy)
- Are leaving after your extension will have expired anyway — e.g. flying out on the last valid day and not planning to return on that extension (chiangmaivisarun)

**Key danger:** The visas that most Pattaya readers hold — **Non-O retirement, Non-O marriage, Non-B with work permit, ED, and Non-O-A** — are all single-entry with in-country extensions. **If you leave without a re-entry permit, your year of effort vanishes.** (youtube, rlcoutsourcing+1)

## Which visas need re-entry permits vs don't

### Visas that DO need re-entry permits

If you plan to leave and return on the same visa/extension, you must get a re-entry permit for:

- **Non-O retirement** (in-country extensions) — 1-year Jomtien extension (pcec+1)
- **Non-O-A retirement** (1-year) — including O-A extensions (thaiembassy+1)
- **Non-O marriage** (Thai spouse) — 1-year Jomtien extension (thailandlawoffice+1)
- **Non-B (business/work permit)** — both 90-day Non-B and 1-year Non-B extensions (rlcoutsourcing, youtube, thailandlawoffice)
- **Non-ED (education)** — student visa holders with 90-day extensions (abroadeduguides+1)
- **Non-M (media)** — 1-year media extensions (pretoria.thaiembassy+1)
- **Non-O-X retirement** — 5-year periods; re-entry permits still required (consular.mfa+1)
- **Embassy-issued 90-day Non-O visas** (if extended in-country) (thaiconsulatela.thaiembassy+1)
- **Single-entry TR tourist visa with extension** — if you want to exit and re-enter during that extension (chiangmaivisarun+1)
- Standard BOI visas unless specifically exempted (BOI often still uses re-entry permits for non-SMART/LTR) (boi+1)

### Visas that DO NOT need re-entry permits

These are multiple-entry by design:

- **Thailand Privilege/Elite visas** — multiple entry; no re-entry permit required (banyangroupresidences+1)
- **LTR visas (all four categories)** — 10-year multiple-entry; holders do not need re-entry permits (thaiembassy+1)
- **SMART visas** — 4-year multiple-entry; no re-entry permit needed (thailand.acclime+1)
- **METV Multiple Entry Tourist Visa** — multiple entry; each entry is 60 days, extendable 30 days (thaiembassy)
- **Visa-exempt entries** — no underlying visa; each exit simply ends the current stay (thaiembassy+1)

## Where to buy re-entry permits

You can obtain a re-entry permit in two places:

1. **Any Thai Immigration office** (e.g. Jomtien for Pattaya) — **before you travel** (rlcoutsourcing+1)
2. **At major international airports on departure** — re-entry counters at:
   - Suvarnabhumi (BKK)
   - Don Mueang (DMK)
   - Chiang Mai (CNX)
   - Phuket (HKT) (rlcoutsourcing+2)

### Option 1: Immigration office (recommended for Pattaya)

- Go to **Jomtien Immigration before your trip**
- Bring:
  - Passport (valid and with current visa/extension stamp)
  - Photocopies (each signed) of:
    - Passport biodata page
    - Visa sticker and/or extension stamp page
    - Latest entry stamp
  - **TM.8 form** (re-entry application form) — can be filled out at Jomtien or printed in advance (immigration+1)
  - One passport-sized photo (4×6 cm) (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
- At the counter:
  - Indicate single or multiple re-entry permit on TM.8 (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
  - Pay 1,000 THB (single) or 3,800 THB (multiple) **in cash** (rlcoutsourcing+1)
- Immigration stamps the re-entry permit into your passport, linking it to your current visa or extension (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
- Time: typically **15–45 minutes** depending on queue (chiangmaivisarun+1)

### Option 2: At the airport (last-minute option)

- Available at major airports (BKK, DMK, CNX, HKT) at **Re-entry Permit counters in the departure area** (thaiembassy+1)
- **Process:**
  1. Arrive earlier than usual — **at least 3 hours before your flight** (thaiembassy+1)
  2. Locate the re-entry permit counter (ask airport staff)
  3. Fill out TM.8, provide passport, copies, and photo (photo booths often available) (chiangmaivisarun+1)
  4. Pay fee, receive passport with re-entry stamp
- **Risk:** queues, staff breaks, or misunderstandings can delay you and risk missed flights. Airport purchase is convenient but **unwise for tight itineraries**. (thaiembassy+1)

## Documents you need (TM.8 checklist)

For all re-entry permit applications (Immigration or airport):

1. **Passport** — with more than 6 months validity in most cases (rlcoutsourcing+1)
2. **Photocopies, each signed:**
   - Passport biodata page
   - Current visa/extension stamp page
   - Latest Thai entry stamp
   - Re-entry permit page (for upgrades, if any) (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
3. **TM.8 form** — application for re-entry permit into the Kingdom (immigration+1)
4. **Passport-sized photo** — 4×6 cm, taken within the last 6 months (rlcoutsourcing+1)
5. TM6 card (if you have one; fewer people now do due to TDAC) (immigration+1)

**No supporting financial documents are required** for a re-entry permit — it is purely an administrative document tied to your existing status. (immigration)

## Validity and limitations

- **A re-entry permit's validity is tied directly to your current visa or extension:**
  - If your 1-year extension expires on 30 September, your re-entry permit is also only valid until 30 September (cmies.eng.cmu+1)
- **A re-entry permit does not extend your stay:**
  - Re-entering with a re-entry permit does not give you another 90 days or another year; you simply resume the remainder of your existing permission (thaiembassy+1)
- Changing your underlying visa status (e.g., switching from Non-O to Non-B) before re-entering can complicate the re-entry permit — always check if a new permit is required

## The forgotten re-entry permit horror stories

These are some of the most expensive mistakes long-stay foreigners in Pattaya make:

1. **Retiree flies back to Europe without re-entry permit:** They return months later, expecting to still have their 1-year Jomtien extension. Immigration treats them as a fresh arrival on a 60-day visa-exempt entry. Their old Non-O extension is dead; they must reseason 800,000 THB for 2 months and redo the entire process. (visa.thainexus+1)
2. **Non-B worker loses work permit and job:** A foreign employee with a 1-year Non-B extension and work permit travels abroad for a wedding without a re-entry permit. On re-entry, the extension and work permit status are destroyed. The employer must restart the Non-B and work permit process, and the employee technically worked illegally until status is fixed. (youtube, rlcoutsourcing)
3. **ED student mid-course blocked from re-entry:** ED visa student goes home during term break, forgets to buy a re-entry permit. On return, ED status is gone; they are admitted as a tourist. Previous ED extensions and course alignment wasted, and they risk being refused a new ED because of poor attendance record once re-enrolled. (abroadeduguides+1)
4. **O-A retiree with insurance loses his visa:** O-A retirement visa holder leaves the country late in his first year without a re-entry permit. Visa gone on departure; on return, he is visa-exempt and must either apply for another O-A from abroad or convert to Non-O in-country — both requiring a lot of new paperwork and insurance checks. (thaiembassy+1)

## 12 FAQ questions

**1. Do I need a re-entry permit if I have a 1-year retirement extension (Non-O)?**
Yes. The 1-year extension is single-entry by default. If you leave Thailand without a re-entry permit, your extension is cancelled. **You must buy a re-entry permit (single or multiple) before any trip abroad.** (visa.thainexus+2)

**2. Do I need a re-entry permit if I hold an LTR visa?**
No. LTR visas are multiple-entry by design. LTR holders can leave and re-enter Thailand freely during their visa validity period without re-entry permits. (ltr.boi+1)

**3. What happens if I leave without a re-entry permit by mistake?**
Your current visa/extension is cancelled on exit. When you return, you are treated as a new arrival (e.g. visa-exempt or new visa). **You must restart the entire long-stay process**, including financial seasoning and new extension application. (youtube, chiangmaivisarun+1)

**4. If I buy a multiple re-entry permit, do I still need to report every 90 days?**
Yes. The re-entry permit only preserves your visa/extension when you leave and come back. **It does not change your 90-day reporting obligation.** You must still file TM47 as usual. (thaiembassy+1)

**5. Should I buy at Jomtien or at the airport?**
The safest practice for Pattaya residents is to buy at Jomtien Immigration **before your trip**. Airport counters are valid but can be crowded or temporarily closed; any delay there threatens your flight. (chiangmaivisarun+1)

**6. Can I get a re-entry permit on the day of my annual extension?**
**Yes — this is a common practice.** Many retirees and Non-B workers renew their 1-year extension and then immediately apply for a multiple re-entry permit on the same visit to Jomtien. (pcec+1)

**7. What if my re-entry permit is in an old passport and I renew my passport?**
You must carry both passports together when you travel — the old one with the re-entry permit/extension and the new valid passport for identity. Alternatively, visit Immigration to transfer stamps to the new passport. (cmies.eng.cmu+1)

**8. Do tourists on a 60-day visa-exempt entry need a re-entry permit?**
No. Visa-exempt entries have no underlying visa to preserve. When you exit, that permission to stay ends; if you return, you start a new 60-day (or whatever is current) visa-exempt period if eligible. (thaiembassy)

**9. I have a single-entry TR tourist visa — do I need a re-entry permit to leave and come back?**
Yes, if you want to reuse that TR visa. Without a re-entry permit, leaving cancels the visa. With a re-entry permit, you can exit and re-enter once (single) or multiple times (if you purchased a multiple permit and have an extension). (chiangmaivisarun+1)

**10. How long before travelling should I apply for a re-entry permit?**
You can apply anytime during your valid stay, but most people do it 1–4 weeks before their trip. **It's fast; there's no minimum lead time, but don't leave it until the last minute**, especially if using Immigration offices with queues. (rlcoutsourcing+1)

**11. Does a re-entry permit give me a new 90-day stay when I come back?**
No. It preserves the **remainder** of your existing permission. If your extension expires in 60 days, you will have only 60 days left on re-entry, not 90. (cmies.eng.cmu+1)

**12. Does Thailand Privilege (Elite) require re-entry permits?**
No. Thailand Privilege/Elite visas are multiple-entry long-term visas. Holders can enter and exit freely within their membership validity period without needing Thai re-entry permits. (banyangroupresidences+1)

## 2025–2026 enforcement & TDAC

- **Enforcement focus, not rule changes:** The underlying re-entry rules have not changed, but 2026 brings stricter enforcement. Immigration now pays more attention to travel patterns, repeated exits, and intent; **leaving without a re-entry permit is less likely to be casually "overlooked."** (facebook, youtube)
- **TDAC (Thailand Digital Arrival Card):** Using a re-entry permit does not exempt you from TDAC. **All non-Thai nationals must submit TDAC within 72 hours before every arrival**, whether they are using a re-entry permit or a new visa. (thailandinsiderguide+1)
- **Better awareness via visa agents:** Major law firms and visa agents now actively educate clients about the "one rule you cannot ignore" — leaving without a re-entry permit. 2025–2026 content from immigration lawyers emphasises this as a key risk area. (youtube, rlcoutsourcing)
